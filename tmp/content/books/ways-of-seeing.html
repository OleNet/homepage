Ways of Seeing — John Berger

2025-11

Berger's central argument is that seeing is never innocent — that how we look at images is conditioned by assumptions we have already made before we look. The image arrives into a context that is already ideological, already shaped.

What I find most useful here is not the critique of art history, though that is sharp. It is the underlying epistemology: that perception is active, not passive; that to see is already to interpret; and that the most powerful images are those that destabilize this interpretive machinery rather than confirm it.

The past is never there waiting to be discovered, to be recognized for exactly what it is. History always constitutes the relation between a present and its past.

Reading this alongside work on generative image systems raises uncomfortable questions. If the training data is an archive — a past — and the model's output is conditioned by that archive, then what is being reproduced is not the image but the relation to the image that was already present in the data. The bias is not in the generation. The bias is in the seeing that preceded it.
