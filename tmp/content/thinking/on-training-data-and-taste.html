On Training Data and Taste

2026-02-18

There is a question that keeps returning when working on generative image systems: what does the model actually learn when we show it a million images labeled "high quality"? It learns correlation, not judgment. It learns to predict which images a certain class of annotators, at a certain moment, under certain conditions, preferred.

This is not nothing. Correlation at scale is useful. But it is not the same as taste, which requires not just preference but reason — the ability to defend a choice in terms of something other than the choice itself.

The practical consequence is that aesthetic models trained on aggregate human preference tend toward the center. They converge on what is broadly pleasing rather than what is precisely right. The more data, the stronger this gravitational pull toward the mean.

The problem of the annotation surface

When we ask annotators to rank images, we are asking them to project a private, partially articulated sense of quality onto a binary or ordinal scale. Something is inevitably lost in this projection. The image that is correct for a reason that takes three sentences to explain will often lose to the image that is pleasant for a reason that takes no sentences at all.

This is a structural problem, not a data problem. Adding more annotators does not solve it. Training a better reward model does not solve it. The surface on which preference is being recorded is too coarse for the thing we actually want to measure.

One possible direction

I have been thinking about whether the right move is not to scale annotation but to stratify it — to find the small number of annotators whose implicit model of quality is coherent and articulate, and to build systems that can learn from their judgments specifically. This is less democratic but possibly more useful.

Whether or not that is tractable at the scale we work at, I do not yet know.
